The brand

Hong Kong's #1 online premium meat and seafood retailer.

Steak King sells premium meat and seafood direct to Hong Kong. Australian Wagyu, USDA Prime, Japanese A5. Five years on Shopify, most revenue from repeat buyers. I run their email and SMS through Klaviyo.

The Meat Emporium

A monthly sale the audience already knows by heart.

Meat Emporium runs every month. About a week, 50+ products, 25-75% off. The audience expects it. Because they've seen it every month, the urgency has worn off. January's Day 1 did HK$325K. February did HK$370K. The owner wanted March to be different.

The challenge

HK$500,000 on day one. No Meat Emporium had ever come close.

Steak King's revenue runs on repeat purchases. The customer base was already in the database. This was a lifecycle problem: how do you get people who've seen this sale every month to spend at a level they never have?

We pitched a strategy built on one idea: create real urgency around a single product, and the rest of the sale follows.

The strategy

A loss leader, manufactured scarcity, and a three-phase lifecycle engine.

1

Pick the product from historical data

We pulled Klaviyo data on every product from past ME campaigns. Rangers Station M3-4 Wagyu Ribeye had the strongest email performance. We recommended pricing it at HK$298 (down from $1,192). Razor-thin margins. The volume would compensate.

2

Hype and tease, two days out

Email 1 revealed the Ribeye at $298: "Not a typo." Email 2 opened up the broader sale. Neither felt like marketing. They read like intel.

3

Launch with scarcity and purchase constraints

200 units at launch. One per order. Minimum cart HK$1,000+. The Ribeye got people to the site. The minimum forced them to browse and fill their cart with everything else.

We had 1,000+ units in stock. We advertised 200. We pulled the product at midnight even with stock remaining, so the next time we say "limited," people believe it.

What happened

Sales started at midnight. Before the announcement even sent.

Orders landed at midnight, eight hours before the launch email. By 8:30am, paired with a WhatsApp blast, the sale was already running. Customers kept coming back through the day, scared the Ribeye would disappear, placing multiple orders and filling their carts each time. 85.7% of all Day 1 orders had the Ribeye. 98.7% had two or more items.

End of day, we sent the last-chance email: 50 units left. At midnight we pulled the Ribeye. Revenue crossed HK$1M around 11pm.

The restock

100 more units on the last day. Sold out in 24 hours.

End-of-sale days see a natural lift in purchase intent. We doubled down on that by dropping 100 Ribeye units at 8am on the final day. Sold out within 24 hours. Full sale: HK$1.78M from 1,373 orders.

Key takeaway

Email was the revenue engine. Not a support channel.

70% of sale revenue came from email. Eight campaigns, 0.11% unsub rate, HK$1.2M in email-attributed revenue. No paid media windfall. No influencer spend. Just the database, Klaviyo, and WhatsApp.

The Ribeye sold out twice, both times in under 24 hours. Find a product the audience wants, price it at the edge of margin, restrict the quantity, build hype. Email does the rest. We'll run this again.
